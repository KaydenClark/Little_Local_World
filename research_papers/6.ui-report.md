# Observer-First UI Patterns from RimWorld and Age of Empires

## Executive summary

For an autonomous-settlement game, **RimWorld contributes the strongest person-level observability**, while **Age of Empires contributes the strongest empire-level glanceability**. RimWorld keeps named colonists persistently visible in a top bar, opens a detailed inspect pane on selection, and exposes internal state through needs, mood, and thought causality. Age of Empires II: Definitive Edition keeps the economy legible through a real-time top resource bar, population and idle-worker indicators, and a bottom command-and-stats strip. Official spectator work in the Age series, especially AoE IV Caster Mode and the officially released CaptureAge tool for AoE II, goes further by turning raw state into comparison bars, queue visibility, efficiency metrics, and high-contrast at-a-glance overlays. citeturn31view0turn15view1turn15view2turn21view4turn28view0turn27view0turn27view1

The design implication is direct: **watching autonomy is a diagnosis problem, not a command problem**. RimWorld is best at answering “who is in trouble, and why?” Age-style HUDs and observer overlays are best at answering “is the settlement winning or losing the macro game?” Neither base game fully solves “what is the local governor trying to do right now?”, which is the biggest missing piece for an observer-first settlement UI. RimWorld fragments that intent across Work, Schedule, Assign, the inspect pane, and alerts; AoE exposes automation in narrow cases such as auto-scout, but not as a persistent local-governor explanation. citeturn17view0turn17view1turn21view3turn27view1

The strongest synthesis is therefore:

- **Use a persistent roster like RimWorld** for agent identity, status, and personal risk.
- **Use a civ-wide strip like AoE and CaptureAge** for resources, labor allocation, capacity, idle counts, and balance bars.
- **Use a right-edge exception stack like RimWorld letters/alerts** for what truly needs attention.
- **Add an always-visible governor card** that states current plan, bottleneck, deviation, confidence, and recent reallocation, because the source games mostly leave that implicit. citeturn31view0turn15view3turn21view4turn27view0turn28view0

## Source frame and analytical lens

This report treats **RimWorld** as the strongest reference for autonomous people simulation and **Age of Empires II: Definitive Edition** as the baseline Age play HUD. For the “watching rather than playing” requirement, it also uses **AoE IV Caster Mode** and the **officially released CaptureAge observer tool for AoE II**, because those are the clearest watcher-oriented UI artifacts in the Age ecosystem. That mix is the most defensible way to study “Age of Empires patterns” for autonomous or spectator-centric settlement watching. citeturn28view0turn21view1turn22view3

Primary-source coverage is uneven. AoE has official learn-to-play UI documentation, official patch notes, official spectator UI posts, and original manuals/reference cards. RimWorld has official marketing/blog references to colonist mood visibility and colonist-bar behavior, but far less official public documentation of the in-game HUD structure, so the community wiki and mod Workshop pages are necessary supplements for concrete UI behavior. I therefore prioritized official pages first, then used community documentation where official sources were thin, and used mod documentation only where it clarified practical information-density solutions already validated by players. citeturn21view4turn21view3turn5view1turn15view5turn15view6turn31view0turn26view2turn15view4

The analytical lens is simple: each UI element is judged by how well it supports four watcher tasks:

1. **Locate the issue quickly**
2. **Understand the cause**
3. **Judge severity and trend**
4. **Decide whether to intervene or keep observing**

RimWorld over-indexes on steps 2 and 3 for individuals. AoE and CaptureAge over-index on steps 1 and 3 for the settlement or match as a whole. A good Local Agent Town screen should deliberately combine both. citeturn15view1turn15view2turn21view4turn27view0turn27view1

## Recommended information density and visibility rules

The core rule is: **keep persistent UI for scanability, not completeness**. RimWorld mods repeatedly add more colonist-bar data because the vanilla bar is useful but under-informative. At the same time, those same mods discuss caching, text-rendering cost, row count, and scaling as colonies grow. CaptureAge’s observer UI reaches a similar conclusion from the opposite direction: put only the highest-leverage aggregates in the always-visible layer, and push richer explanations into tooltips, drill-downs, and alternate dashboard pages. citeturn26view3turn26view4turn29view0turn30view0turn27view0turn27view1turn27view2

### Placement rationale

This layout intentionally combines **AoE’s top-of-screen macro scan** with **RimWorld’s persistent people-awareness**. The civ strip at the top is there because the Age series proves that resources, population, idle counts, and macro state are fastest to read when they sit in a stable, always-visible top bar. The roster sits directly beneath it because RimWorld proves that named actors remain important even in a large simulation, especially when they are grouped and clickable. citeturn21view4turn23view2turn31view0turn15view5

The right side is split between **exceptions** and **selection detail** because RimWorld’s event letters/alerts are already a strong right-edge emergency model, while the inspect pane is the correct place for causality. Keeping both on the right preserves the map as the dominant visual field and makes “what demands attention?” and “why is this pawn failing?” adjacent tasks. citeturn15view3turn31view0

The governor card sits at the bottom-left because it is conceptually closer to “macro policy” than to “map object.” It should read like a short situation report, not like a settings menu. This is the main addition the source games imply but do not fully implement. AoE’s auto-scout button and allied-AI commands show the value of automation, but not its persistent explanation; RimWorld’s Work and Schedule systems expose policy but not current intent. The governor card closes that gap. citeturn21view3turn5view1turn17view0turn17view1

### Interaction rules

Single-clicking a roster chip should center the map and open the selected-agent sheet. Double-clicking should toggle follow-camera. Hover should show a lightweight card with current task, next task, dominant need, and top alert if any. That mirrors RimWorld’s jump-to-colonist utility while respecting the observer-first need for quick diagnosis. citeturn31view0turn26view3turn30view0

Clicking an exception should center the event and pin the related agent, room, or district. Shift-click should keep the current camera but pin the item in the sheet. This borrows the spirit of RimWorld’s alert flow and AoE’s minimap/event navigation without forcing mode changes. citeturn15view3turn23view0turn5view1

The governor card should support only three interactions from the main screen: open decision log, pin current plan on the map, and suspend local autonomy. Anything more belongs in a drill-down. That keeps the main screen observer-first rather than slipping back into micromanagement. This is an inference from AoE IV Caster Mode’s deliberate suppression of distracting controls and from the fact that rich action palettes in both source games live in contextual or secondary UI. citeturn28view0turn23view2turn17view0

## Accessibility and scalability

AoE IV Caster Mode explicitly states that it uses **high contrast and big fonts** to support viewers on small screens and low bitrates. That is a strong primary-source signal for observer-first UI. RimHUD separately notes that it is designed for higher resolutions but still supports lower resolutions by adjusting font size, which reinforces the need for scalable typography and layout modes. citeturn28view0turn26view2

Do not rely on color alone. RimWorld colonist-bar mods repeatedly add **outline boxes, illness icons, bleeding icons, thermal icons, relationship icons, and death timers** on top of mood color, precisely because color-only encoding misses urgency and accessibility needs. For a Local Agent Town UI, every critical state should have **two channels minimum**, usually color plus icon, or color plus motion, or color plus position/order. citeturn29view0turn30view0

For large settlements, the main scaling risk is not only cognition, but rendering cost. RimWorld mod authors explicitly talk about caching, performance faster than vanilla, row count, hidden groups, and text-rendering costs, and they note that scaling problems become more severe as more colonists are shown. That argues for a hierarchy of **group row -> pawn chip -> hover card -> inspector**, not a flat always-on data wall. citeturn26view3turn26view4turn29view0turn30view0

A practical scaling policy is:

- collapse roster by work group or district beyond about **30-40 agents**,
- switch from full chips to **compact chips** beyond about **60 agents**,
- show only flagged agents and pinned favorites in the top row beyond about **100 agents**,
- keep full detail available through search, filters, and the inspector.

That exact thresholding is a design recommendation rather than a source claim, but it follows directly from the performance and grouping patterns documented in the RimWorld mod ecosystem and from spectator tools in the Age ecosystem that prioritize aggregation over full per-unit persistence at large scale. citeturn26view4turn30view0turn27view0turn27view1

The final accessibility recommendation is to separate **state**, **cause**, and **actionability** visually. State belongs in bars, icons, and counts. Cause belongs in signed factor lists and tooltips. Actionability belongs in the alert stack and intervention strip. That separation is one of the clearest shared lessons across RimWorld’s needs/thoughts model and Age observer overlays. citeturn15view1turn32view0turn27view1turn28view0
