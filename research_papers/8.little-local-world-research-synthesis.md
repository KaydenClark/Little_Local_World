# Little Local World Research Synthesis

## Executive thesis

The seven research papers say one thing when combined: Little Local World should be an observer-first autonomous civilization sim, not seven separate feature tracks. The player should watch a deterministic local town, understand why pawns and the governor are making choices, and intervene with light policy changes rather than constant direct control.

The research does not say "copy RimWorld," "copy Townsmen," or "copy Age of Empires." It says to take the strongest transfer from each:

- RimWorld gives the best model for pawn needs, mood, work priority, reservations, and personal causality.
- Townsmen gives the best model for coupled economy loops: needs, logistics, labor, services, treasury, and trade.
- Age of Empires gives the best model for at-a-glance readability, stable macro HUDs, deterministic batching, and disciplined simplification at scale.

For the current project, that means food was the right first deep system because it proves conservation, interruption, mood causality, and supply pressure. But after the current bread retune, the next leverage is not deeper food. The next leverage is autonomous work priorities plus reservations, because that is where the game starts feeling like pawns are living inside a town instead of being hourly counters.

## Topic 1: Mood is a causality ledger, not a work-speed knob

The mood paper says the project needs a per-pawn emotional ledger with two layers: target mood from active thoughts, and current mood drifting toward that target. The important gameplay is not "happy pawn works faster." RimWorld's core lesson is sharper: bad needs and bad events create thoughts, thoughts move mood, low mood creates break risk, breaks create downtime, and Catharsis prevents break chaining.

For Little Local World, the implementation rule is:

- Keep mood on a 0-100 scale.
- Preserve mood target versus current mood.
- Keep hunger, rest, recreation, traits, and Catharsis as explainable ledger entries.
- Do not use generic high mood as a universal productivity multiplier.
- Let productivity change directly through hunger, break state, collapse, injuries, or explicit later inspiration mechanics.

Project decision: the current removal of the generic `mood_factor` productivity boost is aligned with the combined research. Mood should explain behavior and risk. It should not secretly tune the economy.

## Topic 2: Hunger is the first conservation proof

The hunger paper says food is not a meal schedule. It is a continuous reserve with threshold-driven eating. Pawns seek food below the eat threshold, food outranks ordinary work, and integer portion sizing creates the real waste or efficiency problem.

For Little Local World, bread is a custom staple, not a RimWorld vanilla fact. The useful project rule is:

- Treat food as nutrition reserve.
- Let pawns interrupt normal work to eat below the threshold.
- Use bread as a small 0.25 nutrition unit.
- Let pawns eat multiple bread units up to the cap.
- Balance production and starting stock so 12 pawns remain survivable.
- Defer lethal malnutrition until the town has enough UI and autonomy to explain why starvation happened.

Project decision: continuing on food was not the wrong path. It was the right first system because it creates a real resource sink and exposes whether production, hauling, eating, and mood are causally connected. The wrong path would be to keep deepening starvation deaths, food variety, spoilage, and disease before pawn autonomy can react to the pressure.

## Topic 3: Autonomy should be lane-based and explainable

The autonomous-pawn paper says Build-2 should not reproduce RimWorld's full think tree. The right design is a small, explicit arbiter with lanes:

- forced or manual jobs
- hard states
- medical rest
- self-care
- emergencies
- normal work
- idle fallback

Inside normal work, manual priority should dominate, then work-type order, workgiver order, target urgency, path cost, and finally skill fit. Skills affect speed and quality, but they should not rescue a job that failed legality, reachability, capacity, priority, or reservation checks.

Project decision: `set_work_priority` plus reservations is the next core feature. It connects the player's policy layer to pawn action and prevents duplicate claims. It also creates the first useful decision trace: why a pawn chose job A, why it rejected job B, and who reserved the target.

## Topic 4: The economy is loops, not chains

The Townsmen economy paper says production chains are not enough. The game gets interesting when chains become coupled loops:

- needs fulfillment
- local logistics
- labor assignment
- service delivery
- storage pressure
- wages and household spending
- taxes and treasury recirculation
- export trade
- repairs and decay

The strongest lesson is that the town should fail in readable bottleneck classes, not mysterious shortages. The paper's most important bottlenecks are upstream shortage, downstream saturation, hauling starvation, service queueing, repair debt, fiscal squeeze, and export overreach.

Project decision: do not jump from bread straight to a full economy. Add essentials in order. After work priorities and reservations, the first Townsmen-style slice should be water as an essential need: Water Well, water reserve, water need pressure, civ readout, and one governor exception. Storage, markets, repairs, wages, taxes, and trade should come after the autonomy layer can assign work and the UI can explain bottlenecks.

## Topic 5: Readability is a simulation contract

The Age of Empires readability paper says the map should not depend on text to carry the first layer of meaning. A good town viewer separates:

- shape
- footprint
- ownership
- state
- urgency

The map should show work, idle state, construction, danger, and storage pressure through silhouettes, layers, outlines, progress bars, badges, and restrained animation. Text belongs in hover, selection, inspector, or HUD panels.

Project decision: the viewer should not wait for a future art pass to become readable. The next visual slices should be practical:

- idle badge after a short delay
- construction ghost, scaffold, and progress bar
- storage warning at about 80 percent and critical at about 95 percent
- danger overlay above selection
- hover outline distinct from selection outline
- fixed inspector for detail

These are not decoration. They are how the player verifies that the simulation is telling the truth.

## Topic 6: The UI is for diagnosis first

The UI paper says autonomous settlement play is a diagnosis problem before it is a command problem. RimWorld is strong at personal state. Age of Empires is strong at macro glanceability. Little Local World needs both, plus a governor explanation layer that neither source game fully provides.

The combined UI should use:

- a persistent pawn roster for identity and exceptions
- a compact civ strip for resources, population, idle count, labor allocation, and alert count
- a right-edge exception stack for urgent issues
- a selected pawn or building inspector for causes
- a governor card for current goal, phase, bottleneck, confidence, and recent reallocation
- a thin status/intervention strip for pause, follow, overlays, logs, and rare interventions

The most important separation is state, cause, and actionability:

- State lives in bars, icons, counts, and badges.
- Cause lives in signed thought lists, rejection reasons, tooltips, and inspectors.
- Actionability lives in the alert stack, governor card, and intervention controls.

Project decision: every autonomy and economy slice should produce UI evidence. If a pawn does not eat, the player should see whether the cause is no food, unreachable food, reserved food, forbidden work, wrong priority, or a stronger lane winning.

## Topic 7: Scale comes from bounded exactness

The scale paper says a deterministic Python/Pygame town should not try to make every agent smart at every distance. Scale comes from exact truth where the player can read it and cheaper opportunity search everywhere else.

At the current 12-pawn scale, keep almost everything exact:

- job reservation
- inventory transfers
- health and need changes
- ownership changes
- short-range movement
- visible interactions
- deterministic event order

But the paper also says to add two foundations early because they prevent expensive rewrites:

- reachability regions before pathfinding
- deterministic update phases for commands, reservations, job indexes, paths, movement, interactions, metrics, and rendering

Later thresholds should be staged:

- around 16-64 pawns: indexed jobs and cadence buckets
- around 64-150 pawns: long-path abstraction and shared routes
- around 150-400 pawns: district work packets and path budgets
- around 400-1000 pawns: offscreen ETA travel, far-needs cadence, simplified avoidance, and visual LOD

Project decision: do not scale population before the 12-pawn truth loop is satisfying. The next scale-friendly work is not multithreading. It is reservations, stable IDs, deterministic phases, reachability filters, and bounded candidate sets.

## The combined design rule

The seven papers collapse into one product rule:

Little Local World should make autonomous causality visible.

That means the town needs real pressures, autonomous responses, and readable explanations. Food creates pressure. Work priorities and reservations create response. Mood, needs, alerts, badges, and the governor card create explanation. Scaling work then preserves the same truth at larger populations by limiting where exactness is spent.

## Roadmap implication

The research-backed order should be:

1. Finish the narrow food correction.
   - Bread is 0.25 nutrition.
   - Pawns can eat multiple bread portions.
   - Default production and starting bread keep the 12-pawn town viable.
   - Mood no longer grants a generic work-speed boost.

2. Build `set_work_priority` and reservations.
   - Add per-pawn work priorities.
   - Add job target reservations.
   - Add lane-based job arbitration.
   - Add decision traces and rejection reasons.

3. Add the first essential economy extension: water.
   - Water Well produces water.
   - Pawns consume water.
   - Low water creates need pressure.
   - Civ strip and governor exceptions surface shortages.

4. Add map readability for current systems.
   - Idle badge.
   - Construction progress.
   - Storage pressure badges.
   - Danger and blocked-job indicators.
   - Hover and selection conventions.

5. Add the governor card and exception stack.
   - Current plan.
   - Bottleneck.
   - Confidence.
   - Last reallocation.
   - Top exception and likely cause.

6. Add scale foundations before bigger populations.
   - Stable deterministic phases.
   - Reachability regions.
   - Job indexes.
   - Bounded candidate sets.

7. Only then deepen the economy.
   - District storage.
   - Market/service delivery.
   - repair debt.
   - wages, household spending, taxes.
   - reserve-aware trade.

## Explicit deferrals

The combined research says to defer these until the core autonomy loop is visible and stable:

- full RimWorld think-tree parity
- full recreation and joy variety
- chemical needs, psyfocus, prisoners, animals, guests, caravans, and combat AI
- lethal starvation and detailed malnutrition timing
- spoilage-heavy food variety
- full private-enterprise economy
- full wages, taxes, and trade before storage and service bottlenecks exist
- large-population approximations before 12-pawn exactness is proven
- text-heavy dashboards before world badges and inspector causality are readable

## Answer to the current strategic question

We are not climbing the wrong path by working on food. Food is the correct first high-stakes system because it links production, consumption, interruption, mood, survival pressure, and civ-level visibility.

But food should now be narrowed and closed for the moment. The next real step is not more food. The next real step is work priorities plus reservations. That is the point where the research says the game begins to feel like the targets we are replicating: pawns choose work for understandable reasons, the town avoids duplicate claims, the governor's policy affects behavior, and the UI can explain what failed.

The short version: finish the bread correction, then move to autonomy. Do not go deeper into starvation or economy complexity until pawns can choose, reserve, and explain their work.
